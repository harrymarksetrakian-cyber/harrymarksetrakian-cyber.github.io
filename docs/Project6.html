ANALYSIS:¶

According to the data gathered and the milestones I selected to look into, a career in which a player hits 500+ home runs is the most likely to land them in the National Baseball Hall of Fame, with a 68.18% (15 of 22 players) success rate. Next, having a career batting average at or over .300 comes in close second with a perfect 66.67% (20 of 30 players) success rate. Lastly, having an .850 career OPS (on-base plus slugging) or higher is the least indicative of whether a player will reach the Hall of Fame or not, with just a 22.58% chance to get in if achieved. I was surprised that having an .850+ career OPS was the least telling of whether a player would make the HoF or not, since the OPS stat really encapsulates a hitter's ability to do many things well at the plate. I was not particularly surprised that being a member of the 500-HR club was the biggest tell that someone would be a Hall of Famer, but I was surprised that only 68% of players who have hit 500+ home runs made the Hall of Fame. I suppose the reason to that is the controversy of the steroid era in baseball, since many players have long been kept from the Hall of Fame for the simple fact that they have been accused of using performance-enhancing drugs (PEDs).

A few things that limit my findings are the fact that Hall of Fame voters are not only looking for one stat, but instead an across-the-board type of player that has many different favorable statistics. Also, there are players that overlap into all three of these categories, so perhaps creating visualizations of compounded milestones (ex. only players with 500+ HRs, a .300+ batting average, and a .850+ OPS) would be a better indicator as to who would make the Hall of Fame. I bet every player in that category is in the Hall of Fame, unless they were tied up with PEDs. As well as that, there are plenty of other individual statistical milestones I could have investigated (ex. players with a career WAR over 82, or players with a career slugging percentage over .500) to get a more in-depth analysis of chances at the Hall of Fame. Going further, I think it would be interesting to do exactly what I mentioned: Compounding milestones and comparing players who check multiple impressive boxes to the numbers in the Hall of Fame. For example, we could look at players who maintained a batting average over .300, an on-base percentage over .400, and a slugging percentage over .500, since a .300/.400/.500 slash line is colloquially referred to as a "Hall of Fame slash line." I would be interested in seeing how true that is!
